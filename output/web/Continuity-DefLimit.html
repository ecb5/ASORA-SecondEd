    ğŸ”—
    And it is probably tempting for you to make the same assumption and skip the discussion you are about to see. Donâ€™t do that. The limit concept is subtle. There is more here than you probably believe and it is worth taking a few moments to think about deeply.
    ğŸ”—
    The statement \(\limit{n}{\infty}{2^{-n}}=0\) has a very precise meaning. It means that as \(n\) increases arbitrarily (\(\rightarrow \infty \)) the elements of the sequence
    \begin{equation*} \left(2^{-0}, 2^{-1}, 2^{-2}, 2^{-3}, \cdots{}\right) \end{equation*}
    get arbitrarily close to zero \((=0)\text{.}\) Notice that it does not mean that \(2^{-n}\) is actually equal to zero for any value of \(n\text{.}\) That is not true.
    ğŸ”—
    You might reasonably ask, â€œOK, but the notation â€˜ \(=0\) â€™ means that something is equal to zero. What exactly is equal to zero?â€ The answer to that question is as obvious as it is unhelpful. It is the limit of \(2^{-n}\) that is equal to zero.
    ğŸ”—
    Suppose the sequence \(\left(a_n\right)_{n=1}^\infty \) converges to \(a\text{.}\) In ChapterÂ 6 we usually said that \(\limit{n}{\infty}{a_n}\) converged to \(a\) rather than saying that \(\limit{n}{\infty}{a_n}\) is equal to \(a\text{.}\) Unfortunately this idea gets rendered notationally as
    \begin{equation*} \limit{n}{\infty}{a_n}=a \end{equation*}
    so when we read it aloud we tend to speak of equality rather than convergence. This is not wrong per se but it can be confusing.
    ğŸ”—
    The limit concept is a deep and very abstract idea and as weâ€™ve just seen the notation weâ€™ve inherited (especially the way the equals sign is used) is more befuddling than helpful, at least at first. We will proceed slowly.
    ğŸ”—
    Speaking very loosely, when we evaluate the limit of the sequence \(\left( a_n\right)_{n=1}^\infty\) we let \(n\) get close to infinity. That is, weâ€™re interested in what happens at the far right side of the sequence, even though there is no â€œfar right side.â€
    ğŸ”—
    The limit concept has always been lurking in the background of Calculus. In his attempts to justify his calculations, Newton used what he called his doctrine of Ultimate Ratios. For example, the ratio \(\frac{(x+h)^2-x^2}{h} = \frac{2xh+h^2}{h} = 2x+h\) becomes \(2x\) ultimately, or at the last instant before \(h\) becomes zero. Newton would have called \(h\) an â€œevanescent quantityâ€ or â€œvanishing quantityâ€ ([4], p. 33).
    ğŸ”—
    You have seen this sort of expression before, usually with a limit symbol in front of it:
    \begin{equation*} \limit{h}{0}{\frac{(x+h)^2-x^2}{h}} = \limit{h}{0}{\frac{2xh+h^2}{h}} = \limit{h}{0}{2x+h}=2x \text{.} \end{equation*}
    But notice that whereas in the limit of a sequence we were letting \(n\) get close to infinity (which is not a number) here we need to let \(h\) get infinitely close to zero (which is a number). Notice that in evaluating the limit of the sequence \((a_n)\) \(n\) only took on integer values whereas in evaluating the limit \(\limitt{h}{0}{\frac{(x+h)^2-x^2}{h}}\) the limit parameter \(h\) is simply close to zero.     ğŸ”—
    Similarly Leibnizâ€™ differentials (e.g. \(\dx{x}\) and \(\dx{y}\)) can be seen as an attempt to get infinitely close to \(x\) and \(y\) respectively, in much the same way that \(h\) does in \(\limitt{h}{0}{\frac{(x+h)^2-x^2}{h}}\text{.}\) Infinite closeness is the idea at the heart of Calculus.     ğŸ”—

    Definition 10.1.3. Limit.

    We say \(\limit{x}{a}{f(x)} =L\) provided that for each \(\eps>0\text{,}\) there exists \(\delta>0\) such that if \(0\lt \abs{x-a}\lt \delta\) then \(\abs{f(x)-L}\lt \eps\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Before we delve into this, notice that it is very similar to the definition of continuity at a point. In fact we can readily see that \(f\) is continuous at \(x=a\) if and only if \(\limit{x}{a}{f(x)} = f(a)\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    There are two differences between DefinitionÂ 10.1.3 and DefinitionÂ 8.1.7 and the differences are related.     ğŸ”—
    The second is that we have replaced
    \begin{equation*} \abs{x-a}\lt \delta \end{equation*}
