    Section 10.2 The Definition of the Derivative and the Mean Value Theorem

    As we have mentioned in Section 3.1 Leibniz invented his calculus differentialis (differential calculus — literally “rules for (infinitely small) differences”) in the 1600s.
    🔗
    In the late 1700s Lagrange tried to provide a rigorous foundation for Calculus by discarding differential ratios like the expression \(\dfdx{y}{x} \) in favor of his own “prime notation” (\(y^\prime \)). Thus it was Lagrange who established functions and limits, rather than the curves and infinitesimals favored by Leibniz and Newton, as foundational ideas of Calculus
    🔗
    When you took Calculus you spent at least an entire semester learning about the properties of the derivative and how to use them to explore the properties of functions so there is no need to repeat that effort here. Instead we will establish a rigorous, formal foundation for the derivative concept in terms of limits.
    🔗

    Definition 10.2.1. The Derivative.

    Given a function \(f(x)\) defined on an interval \((a,b)\) we define
    \begin{equation*} f^\prime(x) = \limit{h}{0}{\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}}.{} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    There are a few fairly obvious facts about this definition which are nevertheless worth noticing explicitly:
    🔗
    1. If the limit \(f^\prime (x)\) exists at \(x\text{,}\) then we say that \(f\) is differentiable at \(x\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. The derivative is defined at a point. If the derivative of \(f(x)\) is defined at every point in an interval \((a,b)\) then we say that \(f\) is differentiable on the interval \((a,b)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Since it is defined at a point it is at least theoretically possible for a function to be differentiable at a single point in its entire domain.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Since it is defined as a limit and not all limits exist, functions are not necessarily differentiable.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Since it is defined as a limit, Corollary 10.1.6 applies. That is, \(f^\prime(x)\) exists if and only if \(\forall \text{ sequences } (h_n),\, h_n\ne 0\text{,}\) if \(\limit{n}{\infty}{h_n}=0\) then
      \begin{equation*} f^\prime{(x)} = \limit{n}{\infty}{\frac{f(x+h_n)-f(x)}{h_n}} \text{.} \end{equation*}
      Since \(\limit{n}{\infty}{h_n}=0\) this could also be written as
      \begin{equation*} f^\prime{(x)} = \limit{h_n}{0}{\frac{f(x+h_n)-f(x)}{h_n}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    If we make the substitution \(y=x+h\) in Definition 10.2.1 we obtain the following equivalent definition, which is sometimes easier to use.
    🔗

    Definition 10.2.2. The Derivative, An Alternative Definition.

    Given a function \(f(x)\) defined on an interval \((a,b)\text{,}\) and a point \(x\in (a,b)\text{,}\) the derivative of \(f\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} f^\prime(x)=\limit{y}{x}{\frac{f(y)-f(x)}{y-x}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 10.2.3.

    Differentiability Implies Continuity
    🔗
    If \(f\) is differentiable at a point \(c\) then \(f\) is continuous at \(c\) as well.
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem 10.2.4.

    Prove Theorem 10.2.3
    🔗
    🔗
    As we mentioned, the derivative is an extraordinarily useful mathematical tool but it is not our intention to learn to use it here. Our purpose here is to define it rigorously (done) and to show that our formal definition does in fact recover the useful properties you came to know and love in your Calculus course.
    🔗
    The first such property is known as Fermat’s Theorem.
    🔗

    Theorem 10.2.5. Fermat’s Theorem.

    Suppose \(f\) is differentiable on \((a,b)\) and \(f\) has an extremum at \(c\in (a,b)\text{.}\) Then \(f^\prime\left(c\right)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Sketch of Proof.

    There are two cases:
    Case 1:
    🔗
    \(f(c)\) is a maximum, and
    🔗
    Case 2:
    🔗
    \(f(c)\) is a minimum.
    🔗
    🔗
    Suppose \(f(c)\) is a maximum so that \(f\left(c\right)\ge f(x)\) for all \(x\in (a,b)\text{.}\) Since \(f\) is differentiable at \(c\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} f^\prime\left(c\right)=\limit{x}{c}{ \frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To show \(f^\prime\left(c\right)=0\text{,}\) we need to show that
    \begin{align*} f^\prime\left(c\right)\le 0 \amp{}\amp{} \text{and}\amp{}\amp{} f^\prime\left(c\right)\ge 0. \end{align*}
    These facts follow from Problem 10.1.12.
    🔗
    The case where \(f(c)\) is a minimum can be handled by looking at \(-f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem 10.2.6.

    Provide a formal proof for Fermat’s Theorem.
    🔗
    🔗
    Many of the most important properties of the derivative follow from what is called the Mean Value Theorem (MVT) stated below.
    🔗

    Theorem 10.2.7. The Mean Value Theorem (MVT).

    Suppose \(f^\prime\) exists for every \(x\in(a,b)\) and \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then there is a real number \(c\in(a,b)\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f^\prime(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}.{} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    It would be difficult to prove the MVT right now, so we will first state and prove Rolle’s Theorem, which can be seen as a special case of the MVT. The proof of the MVT will then follow easily.
    🔗
    Michel Rolle (1652–1719) first stated the following theorem in 1691. Given this date and the nature of the theorem it would be reasonable to suppose that Rolle was one of the early developers of Calculus but this is not so. In fact, Rolle was disdainful of both Newton and Leibniz’s versions of Calculus, once deriding them as a collection of “ingenious fallacies.” It is a bit ironic that his theorem is so fundamental to the modern development of the Calculus he ridiculed.
    🔗

    Theorem 10.2.8. Rolle’s Theorem.

    Suppose \(f^\prime\) exists for every \(x\in(a,b)\text{,}\) \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\text{,}\) and
    \begin{equation*} f(a)=f(b) \text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Then there is a real number \(c\in(a,b)\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f^\prime(c)=0 \text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Sketch of Proof.

    By the EVT, we know that \(f\) has a maximum \(M\text{,}\) and a minimum \(m\text{,}\) on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Suppose that both occur at the endpoints. This would say that \(m=M\) and \(f\) is constant on \([a,b]\text{.}\) What does this say about \(f^\prime\text{?}\)
    🔗
    On the other hand, what does Fermat’s Theorem say if one or both of these extrema is not at an endpoint?
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem 10.2.9. Rolle’s Theorem.

    Turn the ideas in the sketch above into a proof of Rolle’s Theorem.
    🔗
    🔗
    We can now prove the MVT as a corollary of Rolle’s Theorem. We only need to find the right function to apply Rolle’s Theorem to. The following figure shows a function, \(f(x)\text{,}\) cut by a secant line, \(L(x)\text{,}\) from \((a, f(a))\) to \((b,f(b))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The vertical difference from \(f(x)\) to the secant line, indicated by \(\phi(x)\) in the figure should do the trick. You take it from there.
    🔗

    Problem 10.2.10.

    Prove the Mean Value Theorem.
    🔗
    🔗
    Notice that the MVT is a generalization of Rolle’s Theorem or, put another way, Rolle’s Theorem is a special case of the MVT.
    🔗
    The Mean Value Theorem is extraordinarily useful. Almost all of the properties of the derivative that you used in Calculus follow more or less directly from it. For example the following is true.
    🔗

    Corollary 10.2.11.

    If \(f^\prime(x) > 0\) for every \(x\) in the interval \((a,b)\) then for every \(c,d\in(a,b)\) where \(d>c\) we have
    \begin{equation*} f(d) > f(c) \text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    That is, \(f\) is increasing on \((a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose \(c\) and \(d\) are as described in the corollary. Then by the Mean Value Theorem there is some number, say \(\alpha\in(c,d)\subseteq(a,b)\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f^\prime(\alpha)=\frac{f(d)-f(c)}{d-c} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since \(f^\prime(\alpha)>0\) and \(d-c>0\) we have \(f(d)-f(c)>0\text{,}\) or \(f(d)>f(c)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem 10.2.12.

    Show that if \(f^\prime(x) \lt 0\) for every \(x\) in the interval \((a,b)\) then \(f\) is decreasing on \((a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 10.2.13.

    Suppose \(f\) is differentiable on some interval \((a,b)\text{,}\) \(f^\prime\) is continuous on \((a,b)\text{,}\) and that \(f^\prime(c)>0\) for some \(c\in (a,b)\text{.}\) Then there is an interval, \(I\subset (a,b)\text{,}\) containing \(c\) such that for every \(x, y\) in \(I\) where \(x\ge y\text{,}\) \(f(x)\ge f(y)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem 10.2.14.

    Prove Corollary 10.2.13.
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem 10.2.15.

    Prove the following.
    🔗
    Suppose that \(f\) is differentiable on some interval \((a,b)\text{,}\) and \(f^\prime \) is continuous on \((a,b)\text{.}\) If \(f^\prime(c)\lt 0\) for some \(c\in (a,b)\) then there is an interval, \(I\subset (a,b)\text{,}\) containing \(c\) such that for every \(x, y\) in \(I\) where \(x\ge y\text{,}\) \(f(x)\le f(y)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Problem 10.2.16.

    (a)

    Suppose \(f(x)\) is continuous on \([a,b]\) and \(f^\prime(x)=0\) on \((a,b)\text{.}\) Show that \(f(x)\) is constant on \([a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    Show that for any \(x, y\in [a,b]\text{,}\) \(x\neq y\text{,}\) \(f(x)=f(y)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Consider
    \begin{equation*} f(x)= \begin{cases} \frac{\abs{x} }{x}\amp \text{ if } x\neq 0\\ 0\amp \text{ if } x=0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Show that \(f^\prime(x)=0\) for \(x\neq 0\text{.}\) Why doesn’t this contradict part (a)?
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Suppose \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are continuous on \([a,b]\) with \(f^\prime(x)=g^\prime(x)\) on \((a,b)\text{.}\) Show that \(f(x)=g(x)+C\) for some constant \(C\) on \([a,b]\text{.}\)
