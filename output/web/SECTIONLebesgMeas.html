    Measure, Outer Measure, and Lesbesgue Measure.

    In our side comment, Size vs. Cardinality we noted that the mental image evoked by the word â€œsizeâ€ isnâ€™t really appropriate when discussing infinite sets because two sets can have the same cardinality even though the first completely contains the second, with some left over. For example \(\ZZ \) contains all of \(\NN{}\text{,}\) and all of the negative integers as well.
    ğŸ”—
    We have a similar problem with the concept of length. Two intervals can have the same length even as one completely contains the other, with some left over. For example, \([0,1]\) contains all of the points in \((0,1)\) in addition to \(0\) and \(1\text{.}\) Moreover â€œlengthâ€ is only applicable to intervals which are a very specific kind of set. We will need to be able to compute the â€œlengthâ€ of more general sets. To avoid confusion we need to use a word other than â€œlengthâ€.
    ğŸ”—
    Historically, the word that has been chosen is measure. If \(S\) is a set of real numbers then \(\mu (S)\) denotes the measure of \(S\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—

    Definition 12.3.1. The Definition of a Measure.

    \(\mu \) is a measure on the real numbers if the following statements are true.
    1. If \(S\) is an interval of length \(l\) then
      \begin{equation*} \mu (S)=l\text{.} \end{equation*}
      (We include intervals with the same left and right endpoint: \(\mu ([a,a])=0\)).
      ğŸ”—
      ğŸ”—
    2. If \(\left( S_i\right)_{i=1}^\infty \) is a sequence of sets in \(\RR \) then
      \begin{equation*} \mu\left( \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty S_i\right)\leq \sum_{i=1}^\infty \mu\left(S_i\right) \end{equation*}
      with equality when \(\left( S_i\right)_{i=1}^\infty \) are disjoint.
      ğŸ”—
      ğŸ”—
    3. \(\mu{}\) is translation invariant. More precisely, if \(S \) is a subset of \(\RR \text{,}\) \(x\) is any real number, and \(S_x=\left\{s+x\left| s\in S \right.\right\} \) then
      \begin{equation*} \mu (S)=\mu(S_x). \end{equation*}
      (\(\mu{}\) is said to be shift invariant, meaning that if you shift every element of \(S\) by some amount \(x\) the measure of the set is unchanged.)
      ğŸ”—
      ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    The purpose of the first and third statements should be clear. Since we are looking to generalize the concept of length the measure of an interval should be its length. For example,
    \begin{equation*} \mu \left( (a,b)\right) = \mu \left( [a,b]\right) = \mu \left( [a,b)\right) =\mu \left( (a,b]\right) =b-a\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and if \(\gamma{}\in\RR{}\)
    \begin{equation*} \mu \left( (a,b)\right) = \mu \left( a+\gamma{},b+\gamma{}])\right) = b-a\text{,} \end{equation*}
    ğŸ”—
    The second statement is the Triangle Inequalty dressed up in the language of sets and measures.
    ğŸ”—
    The conditions given in DefinitionÂ 12.3.1 are modeled on properties of lengths (of intervals) but if thatâ€™s all we are interested in there is no need to make this definition.     ğŸ”—

    The Lesbegue Integral.

    A primary objective of this text is to convey to you, the student, an appreciation for, and an understanding of, the need for rigor. That objective remains in force. However, in this section we are at the edge of very deep mathematical waters. Until we start the next section we will only be wading in the shallows. We will thus relax our relentless emphasis on rigor for now.
    ğŸ”—
    For the rest of this section we will set rigor to the side and instead appeal to your intuition by the use of sketches. We will also reason by analogy rather than precisely (e.g., using \(\eps\)) and \(\delta{}\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    In addition, for the remainder of this section we will assume that all of the functions we will consider are positive so that we can safely engage your strong intuitive understanding of area. We do this because our goal here is only to give you a sense of how Lesbegue broadened Cauchyâ€™s definition of the integral. In particular we will not develop Lebesgueâ€™s integral in detail. Should you desire a deeper understanding than youâ€™ll get here a second course in Real Analysis is highly recommended.
    ğŸ”—
    Recall from ProblemÂ 10.4.10 that the Dirichlet Function
    \begin{equation*} D(x)= \begin{cases} 0, \amp \text{ if } x \text{ is rational}\\ 1, \amp \text{ if } x \text{ is irrational} \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    is not Riemann (Cauchy, Darboux) integrable. Dirichlet invented his function as an example of a nonâ€“integrable function. For our purposes it has no importance beyond that. However the existence of nonâ€“(Riemann)integrable functions suggests the question: Can the integral be defined in such a way as to capture all of the intuitive features of (Riemann) integration known to \(18\)th century mathematicians and which also allows us to integrate something as seemingly bizarre as the Dirichlet Function?
    ğŸ”—
    The answer, of course, is yes. Otherwise we wouldnâ€™t have asked. Lebesgue defined such an integral at he turn of the twentieth century.
    ğŸ”—
    Speaking very loosely, if we want to compute \(\int_a^bf(x)\dx{x} \) where \(f(x)\ge 0\) using the Riemann (Cauchy) integral we partition the \(x-\)axis into adjacent intervals with width \(\dx{x}\) and then construct (infinitesimal) rectangles with area
    \begin{equation*} f(x)\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    from each differential. Summing these areas â€” computing \(\int_a^b f(x)\dx{x} \) â€” provides the value of the definite integral.
    ğŸ”—
    Lebesgueâ€™s idea was to find all rectangles with a common height first, gather them together, and sum those areas. In letter to a colleague he described his process as follows:
    ğŸ”—
    â€œI have to pay a certain sum, which I have collected in my pocket. I take the bills and coins out of my pocket and give them to the creditor in the order I find them until I have reached the total sum. This is the Riemann integral. But I can proceed differently. After I have taken all the money out of my pocket I order the bills and coins according to identical values and then I pay the several heaps one after the other to the creditor. This is my integral.â€
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    To see how this worked suppose we want to integrate \(f(x)=\abs{\sin x}\) from \(x=0\) to \(x=\pi{}\) as seen in FigureÂ 12.3.12 below.
    ğŸ”—
    In a Riemann integral we start at \(x=0\text{,}\) compute the first blue area, add to that the first green area, and so on, until we reach \(x=2\pi \) where we add in the last blue area. Notice that area of the first blue rectangle is the height of \(f(x)\) multiplied by the measure of interval on the \(x-\)axis which corresponds to the interval \(\left[ \frac17, \frac27 \right]{}\) on the \(y-\)axis. Weâ€™ll designate that area with \(\mu{(1)}\text{.}\) Similarly \(\mu (2)\) represents the measure of the base of the first green rectangle, and so on for all \(26\) regions.     ğŸ”—
    But nothing says we must add the areas in this order. This is just a convention. Lebesgueâ€™s ideas was to compute the area of all of the blue regions together, then all of the green regions, then the orange, and so on. By rearranging the sum this way the total are of the blue regions is,
    \begin{equation*} f(1/7)\mu (1) +f(1/7)\mu (13) +f(1/7)\mu (14) +f(1/7)\mu (28) \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} f(1/7)\underbrace{\left[ \mu (1) +\mu (13) +\mu (14) +\mu(28)\right]}_{=\Lambda_b} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    The expression \(\left( \mu (1) +\mu (13) +\mu (14) +\mu(28)\right)\) then represents the total measure of the bases on the \(x-\)axis of the blue regions which we designate \(\Lambda_b\text{.}\) \(\Lambda_b\) is the Lebesgue measure of the base of all of the blue regions.     ğŸ”—
    Our integral is thus

    ğŸ”—
